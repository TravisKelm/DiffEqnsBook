    Section 2.4 Linear Differential Equations and Applications

    Subsection 2.4.1 Motivating Example: Evaporating Raindrop [cross-reference to target(s) "Zill" missing or not unique]

    As a raindrop falls, it evaporates to some extent. We assume that as it evaporates, it still maintains its spherical shape. We make the following additional assumptions:
    🔗
    • the evaporation rate of the raindrop is proportional to its surface area; and
      🔗
      🔗
    • air resistance does not impact the velocity of the raindrop.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    With these assumptions, the velocity \(v(t)\) of the raindrop satisfies the model
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dv}{dt}- \left(\dfrac{3\left(\frac{k}{\rho}\right)}{r_0 - \left(\frac{k}{\rho}\right)t}\right)v = g, \ v(0) = v_{0} \end{equation*}
    where \(g\) is the acceleration due to gravity, \(\rho\) is the density of the raindrop, \(r_{0}\) is the initial radius of the drop, and \(k>0\) is the constant of proportionality. We take the downward direction to be positive.
    🔗
    This equation is a linear differential equation. If we assume that
    🔗
    • the initial radius of the drop is \(1\) cm,
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\rho = 1 \text{ kg/}\text{m}^{3}\) (or \(\rho = 0.001 \text{ g/}\text{cm}^{3}\) ), and
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(v_{0} = 0\text{,}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    then we have
    \begin{equation*} v = \dfrac{73500kt^2-49t}{1500000kt-500}\text{ cm/s}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.2 Definition and Examples

    Definition 2.4.1. Linear Differential Equation.

    A first-order differential equation of the form
    \begin{equation*} y'+p(x)y=q(x) \end{equation*}
    with the coefficient function \(p(x)\) and the right-hand-side function \(q(x)\text{,}\) both continuous on an open interval \(I\text{,}\) is called a linear first-order differential equation.
    🔗
    If \(q(x)\equiv 0\) on \(I\text{,}\) the differential equation is called homogeneous and nonhomogeneous otherwise.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.2.

    \(\dfrac{dx}{dt}=kx\) ( \(k\in \mathbb{R}\) ) is linear and separable (autonomous).
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.3.

    \(y'=(\cot x)y\) is linear and separable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.4.

    \(y'=y+x\) is linear, but not separable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.5.

    \(\dfrac{dy}{dt}=ky\left(1-\dfrac{y}{N}\right)\) where \(k,N\in\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) is nonlinear, separable (and autonomous).
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.6.

    \(y'=xy^{2}+\ln x\) is neither linear nor separable.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.3 Method of Integrating Factor

    This method is for solving a linear first-order differential equation
    \begin{equation*} y'+p(x)y=q(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    It works by multiplying both sides by a suitable function \(\mu(x)\text{,}\) called an integrating factor,
    \begin{equation*} \mu(x)y'+p(x)\mu(x)y=\mu(x)q(x) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    to make the left-hand side of the resulting equation recognizable as the derivative of a product:
    \begin{equation*} \left[\mu(x)y\right]'=\mu(x)y'+\mu'(x)y. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    As we can see, the appropriate choice of \(\mu(x)\) is prescribed by the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} \mu'(x)=p(x)\mu(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solving this DE by the method of separation of variables, we have
    \begin{equation*} \mu(x)=e^{\int p(x)\,dx}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    How It Works.

    The method works as follows:
    🔗
    1. Calculate the integrating factor
      \begin{equation} \mu(x)=e^{\int p(x)\,dx}.\tag{2.4.1} \end{equation}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Multiply both sides of the differential equation by \(\mu(x)\)
      \begin{equation*} \mu(x)y'+p(x)\mu(x)y=\mu(x)q(x) \end{equation*}
      🔗
      and recognize the left-hand side of the resulting differential equation as the derivative of a product to obtain the equation
      \begin{equation*} \left[\mu(x)y\right]'=\mu(x)q(x). \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Integrate both sides of the equation with respect to \(x\) to obtain
      \begin{equation*} \mu(x)y=\int\mu(x)q(x)\,dx. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Solve for \(y\) to obtain the general solution of the original equation:
      \begin{equation} y(x) =\dfrac{1}{\mu(x)}\int\mu(x)q(x)\,dx.\tag{2.4.2} \end{equation}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Warning 2.4.7.
    You should not memorize formula (2.4.2). Rather, you should memorize the formula for the integrating factor (2.4.1) and understand how to apply the integrating factor as in Example 2.4.9 through Example 2.4.12.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 2.4.8. Some Notes about Linear Equations.

    1. The general solution of a first-order linear differential equation on an interval \(I\) contains all of its particular solutions, and thus, there are no singular ones.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. It should be noted that the integrals we obtain in the described method may be difficult, or even impossible, to express in terms of elementary functions.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. In the formula for the integrating factor (2.4.1), we can regard the integration constant in the integral \(\int p(x)\,dx\) to be \(0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Given an initial value problem
      \begin{equation} y'+p(x)y=q(x),\ y(x_{0})=y_{0},\tag{2.4.3} \end{equation}
      🔗
      where \(x_{0}\) is in an open interval \(I\text{,}\) where the functions \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) are continuous, we can find its unique solution by first finding the general solution using the method of integrating factor and then finding the appropriate value of the constant \(c\) by using the initial condition.
      🔗
      We can also find the solution of initial value problem (2.4.3) by adjusting formula (2.4.2) for the general solution of the differential equation as follows:
      \begin{align} \mu(x)\amp = e^{\int_{x_0}^x p(t)\,dt} \notag\\ y\amp =\dfrac{1}{\mu(x)}\left[y_{0}+\int_{x_0}^{x} \mu(t)q(t)\,dt\right] \tag{2.4.4}\\ \amp =e^{-\int_{x_0}^x p(t)\,dt}\left[y_{0}+\int_{x_0}^{x} e^{\int_{x_0}^x p(t)\,dt}q(t)\,dt\right]. \notag \end{align}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.9.

    Find the general solution of
    \begin{equation*} y'+3y=2xe^{-3x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since the equation is in the proper form, we first find the integrating factor:
    \begin{equation*} p(x) = 3, \text{ so }\mu(x) = e^{\int 3 \, dx}= e^{3x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Multiplying both sides of the DE by \(\mu(x)\) gives
    \begin{equation*} e^{3x}y'+3e^{3x}y=2x, \end{equation*}
    🔗
    which we can recognize as
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(e^{3x}y\right)=2x. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Integrating both sides with respect to \(x\text{,}\) we have:
    \begin{align*} \int \dfrac{d}{dx}\left(e^{3x}y\right) \,dx\amp =\int 2x\,dx \\ e^{3x}y\amp = x^{2}+c\quad (c\in \mathbb{R}) \text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Solving for \(y\text{,}\) we obtain the general solution of the DE
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{y=e^{-3x}(x^2+c)\quad (c\in \mathbb{R})} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    on \((-\infty,\infty)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.10.

    Find a general solution of
    \begin{equation*} (x^{2}+1)y' + 3xy = 6x. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, we need to divide both sides of the equation by \(x^{2}+1\) to get the equation in the proper form:
    \begin{equation*} y' + \frac{3x}{x^{2}+1}y = \frac{6x}{x^{2}+1}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Find the integrating factor:
    \begin{equation*} p(x) = \frac{3x}{x^{2}+1}, \text{ so }\mu(x) = e^{\int \frac{3x}{x^{2}+1}dx}= e^{\frac{3}{2}\ln(x^2+1)}= (x^{2}+1)^{\frac{3}{2}}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Multiply both sides by \(\mu(x)\) and solve:
    \begin{align*} (x^{2}+1)^{\frac{3}{2}}y' + \frac{3x}{x^{2}+1}(x^{2}+1)^{\frac{3}{2}}y\amp = \frac{6x}{x^{2}+1}(x^{2}+1)^{\frac{3}{2}} \\ (x^{2}+1)^{\frac{3}{2}}y' + 3(x^{2}+1)^{\frac{1}{2}}y\amp = 6x(x^{2}+1)^{\frac{1}{2}} \\ \frac{d}{dx}\left((x^{2}+1)^{\frac{3}{2}}y\right)\amp = 6x(x^{2}+1)^{\frac{1}{2}} \\ \int \frac{d}{dx}\left((x^{2}+1)^{\frac{3}{2}}y\right) dx\amp = \int 6x(x^{2}+1)^{\frac{1}{2}}dx \\ (x^{2}+1)^{\frac{3}{2}}y\amp = 2(x^{2} + 1)^{\frac{3}{2}}+ c \\ \amp \hspace{-3mm}\boxed{y(x) = 2 + c(x^2+1)^{-\frac{3}{2}}\quad (c \in \mathbb{R}).} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.11.

    Solve the initial value problem
    \begin{equation*} y' - y = \frac{8}{11}e^{-\frac{x}{3}}, \ y(0) = -1. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    • First, find the general solution.
      🔗
      • Find the integrating factor:
        🔗
        \begin{equation*} p(x) = -1, \text{ so }\mu(x) = e^{\int -1 dx}= e^{-x}. \end{equation*}
        🔗
        🔗
      • Multiply both sides of the equation by \(\mu(x) = e^{-x}\) and solve.
        \begin{align*} e^{-x}y'-e^{-x}y\amp = \frac{8}{11}e^{-\frac{4x}{3}} \\ \frac{d}{dx}\left(e^{-x}y\right)\amp = \frac{8}{11}e^{-\frac{4x}{3}} \\ \int \frac{d}{dx}\left(e^{-x}y\right) dx\amp = \int \frac{8}{11}e^{-\frac{4x}{3}}dx \\ e^{-x}y\amp = -\frac{6}{11}e^{-\frac{4x}{3}}+ c \\ y\amp = -\frac{6}{11}e^{-\frac{x}{3}}+ ce^{x}. \end{align*}
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    • Find \(c\text{,}\) using \(y(0)=-1\text{:}\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} -1 = -\frac{6}{11}+ c, \text{ so }c = -\frac{5}{11}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{y(x) = -\frac{6}{11} e^{-\frac{x}{3}} - \frac{5}{11} e^x.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.12.

    Solve the initial value problem
    \begin{equation} \dfrac{dy}{dt}=\dfrac{2y+1}{t},\ y(1)=0.\tag{2.4.5} \end{equation}
    Note this is equation is both linear and separable (we solved it in the previous section). The equation (2.4.5) can be separated as follows:
    \begin{equation*} y'-\dfrac{2}{t}\;y=\dfrac{1}{t} \end{equation*}
    However, since the equation was initially presented in the standard linear form, in this example we will solve the original equation (2.4.5) using an integrating factor.
    🔗
    Solution.
    • First, find the general solution.
      🔗
      • Since the equation is in the proper form, we first find the integrating factor:
        🔗
        \begin{equation*} p(t) = -\frac{2}{t}\text{ so }\mu(t) = e^{-\int \frac{2}{t} \, dt}= e^{-2\ln |t|}= t^{-2}. \end{equation*}
        🔗
        🔗
      • Multiplying both sides of the DE by \(\mu(t)\) gives
        \begin{equation*} t^{-2}y'-2t^{-3}y=t^{-3}, \end{equation*}
        🔗
        which we recognize as
        \begin{equation*} \left[t^{-2}y\right]'=t^{-3}. \end{equation*}
        🔗
        🔗
      • Integrate both sides with respect to \(t\) to obtain:
        \begin{align*} \int\left[t^{-2}y\right]'\,dt\amp = \int t^{-3}\,dt \\ t^{-2}y\amp = -\dfrac{1}{2}t^{-2}+c \quad (c\in \mathbb{R}). \end{align*}
        🔗
        🔗
      • Solving for \(y\text{,}\) we find the general solution of the DE:
        \begin{equation*} y=ct^{2}-\dfrac{1}{2}\quad (c\in \mathbb{R}) \end{equation*}
        🔗
        on each of the intervals \((-\infty,0)\text{,}\) \((0,\infty)\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    • We determine \(c\) by plugging in the initial condition
      \begin{equation*} 0 = y(1) = c(1)^{2} - \dfrac{1}{2}, \end{equation*}
      🔗
      so \(c = \dfrac{1}{2}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      Therefore, the solution of the IVP is
      \begin{equation*} \boxed{y(t) =\dfrac{1}{2}t^2-\dfrac{1}{2},\ 0<t<\infty.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    We can also solve this problem by using the definite integral approach (see (2.1.2) ):
    \begin{equation*} \mu(t)=\exp{\left(\int_{1}^t \left[-\dfrac{2}{x}\right]\,dx\right)}=\exp\left(-2\ln|t|\right)=t^{-2},\quad 0<t<\infty. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    and the desired solution on \((0,\infty)\) is obtained from
    \begin{align*} y\amp =\dfrac{1}{t^{-2}}\left[0+\int_{1}^{t} x^{-2}\dfrac{1}{x}\,dx \right] \\ \amp =t^{2}\left[\int_{1}^{t} x^{-3}\,dx \right] \\ \amp =t^{2}\left[\left.-\dfrac{1}{2}x^{-2}\right|_{1}^{t} \right] \\ \amp =t^{2}\left[\dfrac{1}{2}-\dfrac{1}{2}t^{-2}\right], \end{align*}
    so
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{y(x) = \dfrac{1}{2}t^2-\dfrac{1}{2}, \ 0 < t < \infty.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.4 Application: Mixing Problem with Variable Volume

    Example 2.4.13.

    A tank initially contains 1,000 gal of water in which is dissolved 20 lb of salt. A valve is opened and water containing 0.25 lb of salt per gallon flows into the tank at a rate of 4 gal/min. Assume that the mixture is kept uniform and drains from the tank at a rate of 5 gal/min.
    🔗
    1. Set up an initial value problem describing how the amount of salt in the tank changes over time.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. At what time is the tank empty?
      🔗
      🔗
    3. How much salt does the tank contain at any time \(t\) before the tank is empty?
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Recall the model for a mixing problem
    🔗
    rate of change in amount of solute = solute inflow rate - solute outflow rate
    🔗
    (i.e., \(y' = \text{rate in}- \text{rate out}\) ).
    🔗
    🔗
    Recall the following.
    🔗
    Each solute inflow rate is found by multiplying the rate at which fluid flows into the container by the concentration of solute:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{inflow rate}= \text{(rate of fluid inflow)}\cdot \text{(concentration of solute)}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Each solute outflow rate is found by multiplying the rate at which fluid flows out of the container by the concentration of solute in the container:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{outflow rate}= \left(\text{rate of fluid outflow}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{\text{amount of solute in container}}{\text{volume of solution in container}}\right). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    Proceed as follows:
    🔗
    1. For our problem, since the rate of fluid flowing into the tank is not equal to the rate flowing out of the tank, the volume of solution at any time \(t\) is not the same as the initial volume, 1000. Let \(v(t) =\) volume of solution in the tank at time \(t\text{.}\) The volume at any time \(t\) is determined by
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \text{volume}= \text{initial volume}+ (\text{fluid inflow rate})t - (\text{fluid outflow rate})t. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Therefore,
      \begin{align*} v(t)\amp = 1000 + 4t - 5t = 1000 - t \\ \text{rate in}\amp = 4 \, \frac{\text{gal}}{\text{min}}\cdot 0.25 \, \frac{\text{lb}}{\text{gal}}= 1 \, \frac{\text{lb}}{\text{min}} \\ \text{rate out}\amp = 5 \, \frac{\text{gal}}{\text{min}}\cdot \frac{y}{1000 - t}\, \frac{\text{lb}}{\text{gal}}= \frac{5y}{1000-t}\, \frac{\text{lb}}{\text{min}} \end{align*}
      🔗
      So, we have
      \begin{equation*} y' = 1 - \frac{5y}{1000-t}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Rewriting in the form for a linear equation, we obtain
      \begin{equation*} y' + \frac{5}{1000-t}y = 1, \ y(0) = 20. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. To determine the time at which the tank is empty, we use
      \begin{equation*} 1000 - t = 0, \text{ or }t = 1000 \text{ min}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Find the integrating factor:
      \begin{equation*} p(t) = \frac{5}{1000-t}\text{ so }\mu(t) = e^{\int \frac{5}{1000-t} \, dt}= (1000-t)^{-5}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Multiply by \(\mu(t)\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} (1000-t)^{-5}y' + 5(1000-t)^{-6}y\amp = (1000-t)^{-5} \\ \int \frac{d}{dt}\left((1000-t)^{-5}y\right) \, dt\amp = \int (1000-t)^{-5}\, dt \\ (1000-t)^{-5}y\amp = \frac{1}{4}(1000-t)^{-4}+c \\ y\amp = \frac{1}{4}(1000-t) + c(1000-t)^{5} \end{align*}
      🔗
      Find \(c\text{:}\) Since y(0) = 20,
      \begin{equation*} 20 = 250 + c, \text{ so }c = -\frac{230}{1000^{5}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      So, we have that for \(0 \le t \le 1000\text{,}\)
      \begin{equation*} \boxed{y(t) = \frac{1}{4}(1000-t) - \frac{230}{1000^{5}}(1000-t)^5 \text{ kg}.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
