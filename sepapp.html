    Section 2.3 Applications of Separable Equations

    Subsection 2.3.1 Radiocarbon Dating

    Carbon-14 is a radioactive isotope of carbon produced in the upper atmosphere by radiation from the sun. Plants absorb carbon dioxide from the air, and living organisms eat plants. The ratio of normal carbon (Carbon-12) to Carbon-14 in the air and living things is nearly constant. When a living creature dies, the Carbon-14 begins to decrease due to radioactive decay. By comparing the amounts of Carbon-12 and Carbon-14, the amount of Carbon-14 that has decayed can be determined.
    🔗

    Subsubsection 2.3.1.1 Mathematical Model

    Let \(y(t) =\) amount of Carbon-14 present at time \(t\) after death.
    🔗
    The model of radioactive decay we’ll use is:
    \begin{equation*} y' = -ky\quad\text{where}\quad k>0. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We need additional information to find \(k\text{.}\) For example, if we have the half-life of Carbon-14 (which is standard information for a radioactive isotope), we can determine \(k\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsubsection 2.3.1.2 Sample Case

    The half-life of Carbon-14 is approximately 5,730 years. Suppose remains have been found in which it is estimated that 30% of the original Carbon-14 is present. Estimate the age of the remains.
    🔗
    Step 1: Solve the IVP
    \begin{equation*} y'=-ky, \ y(0)=y_{0}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Note: We can also set \(y(0) = 100\) (for 100%) or \(y(0) = 1\) (for 100% in decimal form).
    🔗
    By separation of variables, we see that the general solution is
    \begin{equation*} y(t) = ce^{-kt}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Using the initial condition, \(c = y_{0}\text{,}\) so
    \begin{equation*} y(t) = y_{0}e^{-kt}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Step 2: Find \(k\)
    🔗
    To find \(k\text{,}\) we will use the half-life of Carbon-14. Since the half-life is the amount of time for half of the sample to decay, we know that \(y(5730)=\frac{1}{2}y_{0}.\) Therefore,
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}y_{0}\amp = y_{0} e^{-k(5730)} \\ \ln \frac{1}{2}\amp = -5730k \\ k\amp = -\frac{\ln\frac{1}{2}}{5730}\approx 0.000121. \end{align*}
    🔗
    Step 3: Determine the age of the remains.
    🔗
    At time \(t_{\ast}\) (the age), we have 30%of the original amount of Carbon-14, or \(y(t_{\ast}) = 0.3y_{0}.\) So, plugging this into our solution for \(y(t)\) will allow us to solve for the age of the remains.
    🔗
    \begin{align*} 0.3y_{0}\amp = y_{0} e^{-0.000121t_{\ast}} \\ \ln 0.3\amp = -0.000121 t_{\ast}\\ t_{\ast}\amp = -\frac{\ln 0.3}{0.000121}\approx 9,950 \text{ years}. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.3.2 Geometric Example

    Find the curve with slope \(\displaystyle -\frac{x}{y}\) through the point (0, 1).
    🔗
    Solution: The differential equation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}= -\frac{x}{y} \end{equation*}
    modeling this problem has the implicit general solution
    \begin{equation*} x^{2} + y^{2} = c. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Substituting \(x=0\) and \(y=1\) into it, we arrive at
    \begin{equation*} 1^{2} + 0^{2}=c, \end{equation*}
    so \(c=1\text{,}\) and therefore, the desired curve is the unit circle
    \begin{equation*} x^{2} + y^{2} = 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.3.3 Mixing Problem

    A typical mixing problem is about the amount or concentration of a solute in a solution with certain flows of solutions with the same solute into and out of a container holding the solution.
    🔗
    Model: Let \(y(t)\) be the amount of solute in the container at any time \(t\text{.}\) Then, we have that
    🔗
    rate of change in amount of solute = total solute inflow rate \(-\) total solute outlow rate
    🔗
    (i.e., \(y' = \mbox{rate in}- \mbox{rate out}\) ).
    🔗
    🔗
    Note: The total solute inflow rate is determined by summing the inflow rates from each source, and similarly for the total solute outflow rate.
    🔗
    Each solute inflow rate is found by multiplying the rate at which fluid flows into the container by the concentration of solute:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{inflow rate}= \text{(rate of fluid inflow)}\cdot \text{(concentration of solute)}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Each solute outflow rate is found by multiplying the rate at which fluid flows out of the container by the concentration of solute in the container:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \text{outflow rate}=\left(\text{rate of fluid outflow}\right) \cdot \left(\frac{\text{amount of solute in container}}{\text{volume of solution in container}}\right). \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Note 2.3.1.

    The resulting DE is a first-order linear equation regardless of whether the rates, concentrations, and volume are constant or variable. When all of the rates, concentrations, and volume are constant, this equation is also separable (and even autonomous).
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.3.2. Mixing Problem with Constant Volume.

    A tank contains \(1000\) L of pure water. A brine containing \(0.05\) kg/L of salt enters the tank at a constant rate of \(5\) L/min. A brine containing \(0.04\) kg/L of salt enters the tank at a constant rate of \(10\) L/min. The solution is kept thoroughly mixed and drains from the tank at a constant rate of \(15\) L/min.
    🔗
    1. Set up an initial value problem describing how the amount of salt in the tank changes over time.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Find the amount of salt in the tank after \(t\) min.
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. Let \(y(t)\) be the amount of salt in the tank (kg) after \(t\) min.
      🔗
      The rate of change of the amount of salt in the tank \(\dfrac{dy}{dt}\) (kg/min) consists of the two components:
      🔗
      • the inflow rate of salt entering the tank, which consists of the sum of the rates coming from the two sources
        🔗
        \begin{align*} \text{rate in}\amp = 5 \, \frac{\text{L}}{\text{min}}\cdot 0.05 \, \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{L}}+ 10 \, \frac{\text{L}}{\text{min}}\cdot 0.04 \, \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{L}} \\ \amp = 0.65\ \text{ kg/min}. \end{align*}
        🔗
      • the outflow rate of salt leaving the tank:
        🔗
        \begin{equation*} \text{rate out}= 15 \frac{\text{L}}{\text{min}}\cdot\dfrac{y \text{ kg}}{1000 \text{ L}}= 0.015y\ \text{ kg/min}. \end{equation*}
        🔗
      🔗
      Since initially there was no salt in the tank ( \(y(0)=0\) ), we obtain at the following IVP model for the mixing problem:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dy}{dt}=0.65-0.015y,\ y(0)=0. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. The DE is separable, and more precisely, it is autonomous. Applying the method of separation of variables, we have:
      🔗
      • The equilibrium solution is \(y=\dfrac{0.65}{0.015}\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      • Separating the variables, we obtain
        🔗
        \begin{align*} \dfrac{1}{0.65-0.015y}\,dy\amp = 1\,dt \\ \int\dfrac{1}{0.65-0.015y}\,dy\amp =\int 1\,dt \\ \dfrac{1}{-0.015}\ln|0.65-0.015y|\amp = t+c_{1} \quad (c_{1} \in \R). \end{align*}
        Now we solve for \(y\text{:}\)
        🔗
        \begin{align*} \ln|0.015y-0.65|\amp = -0.015t+c \quad (c \in \R) \\ y(t)\amp = \frac{0.65}{0.015}+ce^{-0.015t}\quad (c \in \R) \end{align*}
        🔗
      • Find \(c\text{:}\) Using the initial condition \(y(0)=0\text{,}\) we have
        \begin{equation*} 0 = \frac{0.65}{0.015}+ c, \end{equation*}
        so \(c=-\dfrac{0.65}{0.015}\) and the solution of the problem is
        \begin{equation*} \boxed{y(t) =\frac{0.65}{0.015}-\frac{0.65}{0.015}e^{-0.015t}.} \end{equation*}
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    Note that \(y \) increasingly tends to the equilibrium \(\dfrac{0.65}{0.015}\approx 43 \) kg as \(t\to \infty \text{,}\) but never attains it.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.3.4 Newton’s Law of Cooling/Heating

    Newton’s Law of Cooling/Heating states that the rate of change of the temperature of an object, \(T\text{,}\) is proportional to the difference between the ambient temperature of the surroundings, \(A\text{,}\) and \(T\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dT}{dt}= k(A - T), \end{equation*}
    where \(k > 0\) is the constant of proportionality.
    🔗

    Example 2.3.5.

    Consider a tray of cookies removed from the oven at 350 \(^{\circ}\) F and placed in a room with a temperature of 72 \(^{\circ}\) F. If the cookies are at 240 \(^{\circ}\) F after 10 minutes, how long will it take for them to cool to 80 \(^{\circ}\) F?
    🔗
    Solution.
    The IVP modeling this situation is given by applying equation [cross-reference to target(s) "eq-tcool" missing or not unique]:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dT}{dt}= k(72 - T), \ T(0) = 350. \end{equation*}
    We will apply the method of separation of variables. Separating variables gives
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{1}{72-T}\, dT = k \, dt. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Integrate both sides and solve for \(T\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \int \dfrac{1}{72-T}\, dT\amp = \int k \, dt \\ -\ln|72 - T|\amp = kt + c \\ \ln|72 - T|\amp = -kt + c \\ 72 - T\amp = ce^{-kt} \\ T\amp = 72 + ce^{-kt}. \end{align*}
    We next need to determine \(c\) and \(k\text{.}\) We first find \(c\) using the initial condition:
    \begin{equation*} 350 = T(0) = 72 + c, \text{ so }c = 278. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    So, we have
    \begin{equation*} T = 72 + 278e^{-kt}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now we use the fact (given in the problem) that \(T(10) = 240\) to find \(k\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} 240 \amp = 72 + 278e^{-10k} \\ 168 \amp = 278 e^{-10k} \\ e^{-10k} \amp = \dfrac{168}{278}= \dfrac{84}{139}. \end{align*}
    Then take the natural log of both sides and solve for \(k\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{align*} -10k\amp = \ln\left( \dfrac{84}{139}\right) \\ k\amp = -\frac{1}{10}\ln\left( \dfrac{84}{139}\right) \approx 0.053657. \end{align*}
    Let \(t_{s}\) denote the time at which the temperature of the cookies is 80 \(^{\circ}\) F. Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 80 = 72 + 278e^{-0.053657 t_s}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solving for \(t_{s}\) gives us \(t_{s} = 70.45\text{.}\) So, the cookies will be at 80 \(^{\circ}\) F after 70.45 minutes, or after 1 hour, 10 minutes, and 27 seconds.
    🔗

      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dt}= -r y, \end{equation*}
      where \(r > 0\text{.}\) Suppose that after 2 hours, half of the initial amount of the drug is left in the patient’s system (i.e., \(y(2) = 0.5y(0)\) ). Find \(y(t)\) assuming that initially, the patient is given 250 mg of the drug (i.e., \(y(0) = 250\) ).
      🔗
      🔗

    6. (Population Model) A new species of animal has just been discovered. The rate of change in the size of its population was found to be proportional to the square root of its current size.
      🔗
      1. Formulate a mathematical model describing the population size \(P(t)\) of this new species over time.
        🔗
        🔗
      2. If the initial population is \(100\) animals and, after 2 months, the population has grown to 400 animals, determine the size of the population at any time \(t\text{.}\)
        🔗
        🔗
      3. How long before the population has grown to 1000 animals?
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    7. (Logistic Population Model) A population model that takes into account the fact that, as a population grows in size its rate of change decreases, is the logistic model. Given a population of size \(P(t)\text{,}\) the logistic model is defined by
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dP}{dt}= rP\left(1 - \dfrac{P}{K}\right), \end{equation*}
      🔗
      where \(r\) is the intrinsic growth rate of the population in a given area (the growth rate in the absence of any restrictions) and \(K\) is the carrying capacity of the area (the maximum population that can be supported).
      🔗
      If \(r = 2\) per year and \(K = 10000\text{,}\) use the logistic equation to determine the population size at any time of a species with an initial population of 1000.
      🔗
      Hint: You will need to use partial fractions to evaluate the integral on the left-hand side.
