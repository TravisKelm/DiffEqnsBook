    Section 2.5 Substitution Method

    Subsection 2.5.1 General Idea

    The general idea of the Substitution Method for a first order differential of the form
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dy}{dx}= f(x, y) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    is to
    🔗
    • introduce a new function
      \begin{equation} v = g(x,y),\tag{2.5.1} \end{equation}
      🔗
      🔗
    • differentiate by the Chain Rule (considering that \(y = y(x)\) )
      \begin{equation} \dfrac{dv}{dx}= \dfrac{\partial g}{\partial x}(x, y) + \dfrac{\partial g}{\partial y}(x, y)\dfrac{dy}{dx},\tag{2.5.2} \end{equation}
      🔗
      and
      🔗
      🔗
    • solve (2.5.2) \(y'\) and (2.5.1) for \(y\) (if necessary) and substitute into the original DE to obtain a new first-order DE for \(v\) that would belong to one of the two classes of DE’s that we know how to solve ( separable or linear );
      🔗
      🔗
    • solve the obtained DE for \(v\text{;}\) and
      🔗
      🔗
    • substitute \(v=g(x,y)\) back into the result to obtain an implicit solution of the original DE for \(y\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    • A "good" substitution \(v=g(x, y)\) simplifies the DE, turning into one we can solve.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Obtaining an explicit solution for \(y\) may be possible but is not always practical.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.1.

    Find a general solution of
    \begin{equation*} (2x\sin y\cos y)y'=4x^{2}+\sin^{2}y. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    Observe that, assuming \(y\) to be a function of \(x\text{,}\) \(y=y(x)\text{,}\) by the chain rule, we have:
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{d}{dx}(\sin^{2} y)=2\sin y\cos y y'. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Hence via the substitution
    \begin{equation*} v=\sin^{2} y,\text{ so }v'=2\sin y\cos y y', \end{equation*}
    🔗
    the DE is transformed into
    \begin{equation*} xv'=4x^{2}+v, \end{equation*}
    🔗
    which we write in standard form is
    \begin{equation*} v'-\dfrac{1}{x}v=4x. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The latter differential equation is linear, so we may solve it using the method of integrating factor. The integrating factor is
    \begin{equation*} \mu(x)=e^{-\int\frac{1}{x}\,dx}=e^{-\ln|x|}=\dfrac{1}{x}\quad \text{(the absolute value can be omitted)}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Multiplying both sides by \(\mu(x)=\dfrac{1}{x}\) and recognizing the left-hand side as the derivative of a product, we have:
    \begin{equation*} \left(\dfrac{1}{x}v\right)'=4. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Integrating both sides with respect to \(x\) gives
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{1}{x}v=\int 4\,dx=4x+c\quad (c\in \mathbb{R}). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solving for \(v\text{,}\) we obtain
    \begin{equation*} v=4x^{2}+cx\quad (c\in \mathbb{R}) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    and substituting back, we obtain an implicit general solution of the original equation,
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\sin^2 y=4x^2+cx \text{ where } (c\in\mathbb{R}).} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.5.2 Homogeneous Differential Equations

    Motivating Example: Solar Collector [cross-reference to target(s) "Zill" missing or not unique].

    If we assume that when the curve \(C\) shown below is revolved about the \(x\)-axis, it produces a surface of revolution having the property that all light rays \(L\) parallel to the \(x\)-axis, upon striking the surface, are reflected to one point \(O\text{,}\) which we label the origin.
    🔗
    
    Ray from the origin reflects parallel to \(x\)-axis when ray hits the curve.
    🔗
    Using the fact that the angle of incidence is equal to the angle of reflection, we can determine a differential equation that describes the shape of the curve \(C\text{.}\) This type of curve is important in applications, such as the construction of telescopes and satellite antennas, automobile headlights, and solar collectors. From geometry, we can see that the angle between the reflected line and the \(x\)-axis, denoted \(\phi\text{,}\) and the angle between \(L\) and \(C\) at the point of incidence, denoted \(\theta\) are related by \(\phi = 2\theta\text{.}\) Using this, we obtain the differential equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}= \frac{-x + \sqrt{x^{2} + y^{2}}}{y}. \end{equation*}
    This equation is nonlinear and homogeneous, where homogeneous will be defined below. The solution of this problem requires a bit more work than the examples which we will do, because it results in a complicated integral. A general solution is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y^{2} = c(c-2x), \end{equation*}
    🔗
    which is a parabola that is symmetric with respect to the \(x\)-axis. Try to see if you can obtain this solution.
    🔗
    Note 2.5.2.
    This problem may also be solved by a different type of substitution, namely, \(u = x^{2} + y^{2}\text{.}\) We will discuss such substitutions a bit later.
    🔗
    🔗
    Definition 2.5.3.
    First-Order Homogeneous Differential Equations
    🔗
    A first-order homogeneous differential equation is a first-order differential equation that can be written as
    \begin{equation} \frac{dy}{dx}= F\left(\frac{y}{x}\right).\tag{2.5.3} \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗
    To solve differential equation (2.5.3), make the substitution
    \begin{equation*} v= \frac{y}{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Therefore,
    \begin{equation*} y= vx\quad \text{and}\quad\frac{dy}{dx}= x \frac{dv}{dx}+ v\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Substituting into (2.5.3) gives
    \begin{equation*} x \frac{dv}{dx}+ v = F(v) \text{ so }x \frac{dv}{dx}= F(v) - v\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is a separable equation.
    🔗
    Example 2.5.4.
    Find a general solution of
    \begin{equation*} x^{3}y'=x^{2}y-y^{3}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    Dividing both sides by \(x^{3}\text{,}\) we see that we obtain:
    \begin{equation*} y'=\dfrac{y}{x}-\left(\dfrac{y}{x}\right)^{3}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, the DE is homogeneous.
    🔗
    Substituting
    \begin{equation*} v=\dfrac{y}{x},\text{ so }y=xv \text{ and }y'=v+xv', \end{equation*}
    we obtain the separable DE
    \begin{equation*} v+xv'=v-v^{3}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The equilibrium solution is \(v=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Separating the variables and integrating, we have:
    \begin{align*} -\int v^{-3}\,dv\amp =\int \dfrac{1}{x}\,dx \\ \frac{1}{2}v^{-2}\amp = \ln|x|+c \quad (c\in \mathbb{R}) \text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    (Note that the singular equilibrium solution \(v=0\) is not included.)
    🔗
    Multiplying by 2 and substituting back, we obtain an implicit general solution of the initial problem
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\dfrac{x^2}{y^2}=2\ln|x|+c\quad (c\in \mathbb{R})} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Note 2.5.5.
    In the previous example:
    • The singular equilibrium solution \(y=0\) is not included.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Since the DE is nonlinear, singular solutions were to be expected.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Example 2.5.6.
    Solve the initial value problem
    \begin{equation*} xyy' = 2y^{2} + 4x^{2}, \ y(2) = 4. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    Divide by \(xy\) to get the equation in standard form:
    \begin{equation*} y' = 2\frac{y}{x}+ 4 \frac{x}{y}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This is a homogeneous equation, so let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} v = \frac{y}{x},\\ \text{ and so }y = vx \text{ and }y' = xv' + v. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Plugging into our DE gives
    \begin{align*} xv'+v\amp = 2v+\frac{4}{v} \\ xv'\amp = v + \frac{4}{v} \\ \amp = \frac{v^{2}+4}{v} \end{align*}
    🔗
    So, we have
    \begin{equation*} x\frac{dv}{dx}= \frac{v^{2}+4}{v}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Then
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{v}{v^{2}+4}dv= \int \frac{1}{x}dx\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Integrating by using the substitution \(u = v^{2} + 4\) for the left-hand integral gives
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}\ln(v^{2}+4)\amp = \ln|x| + c_{1} \\ \ln(v^{2} + 4)\amp = 2 \ln|x| + c_{2} \\ e^{\ln(v^2+4)}\amp = e^{2\ln|x| + c_2} \\ (v^{2}+4)\amp = cx^{2} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Substituting back in \(\displaystyle v = \frac{y}{x}\) gives
    \begin{align*} \left(\frac{y}{x}\right)^{2} + 4\amp = cx^{2}, \amp\text{or}y^{2} + 4x^{2}\amp = cx^{4} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Find \(c\text{:}\) Since \(y(2) = 4\text{,}\) we have \(4^{2} + 4(2)^{2} = c(2)^{4}\text{,}\) so \(c = 2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    So we obtain the unique solution
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{y^2 + 4x^2 = 2x^4.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.5.3 Another Substitution

    One example of another type of substitution is for equations of the form
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dy}{dx}= F(ax + by + c), \end{equation*}
    🔗
    where \(a, \, b, \text{ and }c\) are constants and \(b \ne 0\text{.}\) This can be transformed into a separable differential equation by the substitution
    \begin{equation*} v = ax + by + c. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Then,
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dv}{dx}= a + b\dfrac{dy}{dx}, \end{equation*}
    🔗
    so
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dy}{dx}= \dfrac{1}{b}\left(\dfrac{dv}{dx}- a\right). \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 2.5.7.

    Find a general solution of
    \begin{equation*} y' = (x+y+3)^{2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(v = x + y + 3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Then
    \begin{align*} v'&= 1+ y' \end{align*}
    Since \(y' = (x + y +3)^{2} = v^{2}\) , this gives
    \begin{align*} v'&= v^{2}+1 \end{align*}
    🔗
    One can observe that this equation has no equilibrium solutions. (Can you see why not?)
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{dv}{v^{2}+1}&= \int dx \\ \tan^{-1}v&= x + c \\ v&= \tan(x+c) \text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Substituting back in \(v = x + y + 3\) gives
    \begin{equation*} x+y+3= \tan(x+c). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Therefore, the unique solution is
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{y(x) = \tan(x+c)-x-3 \quad (c \in \mathbb{R}).} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.8.

    Find a general solution of
    \begin{equation*} y'=x^{2}-2xy+y^{2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    Rewriting the DE as
    \begin{equation*} y'=(y-x)^{2} \end{equation*}
    and substituting
    \begin{equation*} v=y-x,\text{ so }v'=y'-1 \text{ and }y'=v'+1, \end{equation*}
    we arrive at the separable DE
    \begin{equation*} v'=v^{2}-1. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The equilibrium solutions are \(v=\pm 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Separating the variables and integrating, we have:
    \begin{align*} \int\dfrac{1}{v^2-1}\,dv\amp =\int 1\,dx \\ \dfrac{1}{2}\ln\left|\dfrac{v-1}{v+1}\right|\amp =x+c \quad (c\in \mathbb{R}) \text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Exponentiating both sides, we obtain
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{v-1}{v+1}=ce^{2x}\quad (c\in\mathbb{R}). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    (Note that the singular equilibrium solution \(v=-1\) is not included).
    🔗
    Next, substituting back ( \(v=y-x\) ), we obtain an implicit general solution of the initial problem
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{\dfrac{y-x-1}{y-x+1}=ce^{2x}\quad (c\in\mathbb{R})}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.5.9. Regarding Example 2.5.8.

    • Note that the singular solution \(y=x-1\) is not included.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Since the DE nonlinear, singular solutions were to be expected.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.5.4 Bernoulli Equations

    Motivating Example: A Falling Chain [cross-reference to target(s) "Zill" missing or not unique].

    A portion of a uniform chain that is 8 feet long is loosely coiled around a peg at the edge of a high horizontal platform, and the rest of the chain hangs at rest over the edge of the platform. Suppose the length of the overhanging chain is 3 feet, that the chain weighs 2 lb/ft, and that the positive direction is downward. Starting at \(t = 0\) seconds, the weight of the overhanging portion causes the chain on the platform to smoothly uncoil and then fall to the ground. If \(x(t)\) denotes the length of the chain overhanging the table at time \(t > 0\text{,}\) then \(v = \frac{dx}{dt}\) is its velocity. If the simplifying assumption of ignoring any resistance forces (e.g., friction), then a model relating \(v\) to \(y\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} xv\frac{dv}{dx}+ v^{2} = 32x. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This differential equation is a Bernoulli equation (to be discussed immediately following). Also, interestingly enough, one can determine an ``integrating factor,’’ which, when multiplied by the equation, turns it into an exact equation (the final type of equation to be discussed). If you solve this equation either way, you can determine that
    🔗
    1. \(v\) satisfies
      \begin{equation*} v(x) = 8\sqrt{\frac{x}{3} - \frac{9}{x^{2}}}, \end{equation*}
      and
      🔗
      🔗
    2. the chain leaves the platform with a velocity of 12.7 ft/s (found by evaluating \(v(8)\text{,}\) since the chain is 8 ft. long).
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.5.10. Bernoulli Differential Equation.

    A Bernoulli differential equation is a first-order differential equation of the form
    \begin{equation} y' + p(x) y = q(x) y^{n}\text{.}\tag{2.5.4} \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.5.11.

    The Bernoulli equation (2.5.4) will be nonlinear so long as \(n \ne 0,1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Bernoulli equations can be solved by transforming them into linear equations. To reduce (2.5.4) to linear form, do a change of variables:
    \begin{align*} \text{Let }v&=y^{1-n} \end{align*}
    Differentiating both sides with respect to \(x\) > gives
    \begin{align*} v'&= (1-n)y^{-n}y' \\ (\text{From \knowl{./knowl/xref/eq-Bernoulli-definition.html}{\text{(2.5.4)}}, }y'&= q(x)y^{n}-p(x)y, \text{ so}) \\ v'&= (1-n)y^{-n}(q(x)y^{n}-p(x)y) \\ &= (1-n)q(x) - (1-n)p(x) y^{1-n} \\ &= (1-n)q(x) - (1-n)p(x)v \end{align*}
    🔗
    We therefore obtain the linear DE
    \begin{equation*} v' + (1-n)p(x)v = (1-n)q(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 2.5.12.

    Find a general solution of
    \begin{equation*} x^{3}y'=x^{2}y-y^{3}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    Solved in the previous example as homogeneous, writing this equation in standard form gives
    \begin{equation*} y'-\dfrac{1}{x}y=-\dfrac{1}{x^3}y^{3}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We can see that this equation is also Bernoulli (with \(n=3\) ).
    \begin{align*} \text{Let }v&= y^{1-n}=y^{-2}. \end{align*}
    Then
    \begin{align*} v'&= -2y^{-3}y' \\ &= -2y^{-3}\left(-\dfrac{y^3}{x^3}+\frac{1}{x}y\right) \\ &= 2x^{-3}- \frac{2}{x}y^{-2}\\ &= 2x^{2} - \frac{2}{x}v, \end{align*}
    or
    \begin{align*} v'&+\frac{2}{x}v = 2x^{-3}, \end{align*}
    which is a linear equation.
    🔗
    The integrating factor is \(\mu(x)=e^{\int\frac{2}{x}\,dx}=e^{2\ln|x|}=x^{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Multiplying both sides by \(\mu(x)=x^{2}\) and recognizing the left-hand side as the derivative of a product, we have:
    \begin{equation*} (x^{2}v)'=\dfrac{2}{x}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Integrating gives
    \begin{equation*} x^{2}v=\int\dfrac{2}{x}\,dx=2\ln|x|+c\quad (c\in \mathbb{R}), \end{equation*}
    🔗
    and substituting back, we obtain an implicit general solution of the initial DE
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{x^2}{y^2}=2\ln|x|+c \quad (c\in \mathbb{R})\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Note that the singular equilibrium solution \(y=0\) is not included.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.13.

    Find a general solution of
    \begin{equation*} xy' - y = 2x^{3}y^{-2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    Divide both sides by \(x\text{,}\) giving:
    \begin{equation*} y' - \frac{1}{x}y = 2x^{2} y^{-2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This is Bernoulli with \(n = -2\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \text{Let }v&=y^{1-n}=y^{3}. \end{align*}
    Then
    \begin{align*} v'&= 3y^{2}y' \\ &= 3y^{2}\left(2x^{2}y^{-2}+\frac{1}{x}y\right) \\ &= 6x^{2} + \frac{3}{x}y^{3} \\ &= 6x^{2} + \frac{3}{x}v, \end{align*}
    or
    \begin{align*} v'&- \frac{3}{x}v = 6x^{2} \text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    We now have a linear DE, so we find the integrating factor:
    \begin{equation*} p(x) = -\frac{3}{x}, \text{ so }\mu(x) = e^{-\int \frac{3}{x} dx}= e^{-3\ln|x|}= x^{-3}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Multiplying by \(\mu(x)=x^{-3}\) gives:
    \begin{align*} x^{-3}v' - 3x^{-4}v&= \dfrac{6}{x} \\ \int \frac{d}{dx}\left(x^{-2}v\right) dx&= \int \dfrac{6}{x}dx \\ x^{-3}v&= 6\ln|x|+c \\ v&= 6x^{3}\ln|x| + cx^{3} \quad (c \in \mathbb{R}) \text{.} \end{align*}
    🔗
    The general solution is then
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{y^{3} = 6x^{3}\ln|x|+cx^3 \quad (c \in \mathbb{R}),} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    or
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{y(x) = \left(6x^3\ln|x|+cx^3\right)^{\frac{1}{3}} \quad (c \in \mathbb{R}).} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
