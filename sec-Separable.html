    Section 2.2 Separable Differential Equations

    Subsection 2.2.1 Motivating Example: Home Loan

    Suppose you want to take out a 30-year loan to purchase a house. If interest rates are 4.75% and you know that you can afford at most \$1,250 per month, you would like to determine the most expensive house that you can purchase in order pay off the loan on time. To do this, define \(L(t)\) as the amount that you owe after \(t\) years. Since you can afford \$1,250 per month, your annual payments will be \(\$1,250 \times 12 = \$15,000\text{.}\) The balance due will gain 4.75% interest, so that amount added to the balance annually is \(0.0475L\text{.}\) Thus, the rate of change in the amount of money due is given by the difference between the amount the loan increases and the amount that you pay on the loan, or \(\dfrac{dL}{dt}=0475L - 15000\text{.}\) The initial condition is \(L(30) = 0\text{,}\) because you want the entire loan to be paid off by the end of the 30 years. This gives the initial value problem
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \frac{dL}{dt}= 0.0475L - 15000, \, L(30) = 0.\tag{2.2.1} \end{equation}
    This is a separable equation, as we will see momentarily (also a linear equation, as we will see in Section [cross-reference to target(s) "sec-Linear" missing or not unique] ). The solution to (2.2.1) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} L(t) = \frac{6000000}{19}\left(1 - e^{\frac{19}{400}t-\frac{57}{40}}\right). \end{equation*}
    The size of the loan you should take is the amount you owe at the beginning, or \(L(0)\text{,}\) which is \$239,839.43.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.2 Definition and Examples

    Definition 2.2.1. Separable Differential Equation.

    A first-order differential equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x,y), \end{equation*}
    whose right-hand side can be factored into \(g(x)h(y)\text{,}\) i.e., it can written in the form
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \dfrac{dy}{dx}=g(x)h(y),\tag{2.2.2} \end{equation}
    is called separable.
    🔗
    There are two special cases of separable equations:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \frac{dy}{dx}\amp =g(x) \quad (h(y)=1),\\ \frac{dy}{dx}\amp =h(y)\quad (g(x)=1) \qquad \text{(called an \emph{autonomous equation})}. \end{align*}
    🔗

    Example 2.2.2.

    1. \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=x^{2}e^{-y}\) is separable.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\dfrac{dy}{dt}=\sin t^{2}\) is separable.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The exponential differential equation \(\dfrac{dx}{dt}=kx\) where \(k\in \mathbb{R}\) is separable (and autonomous).
      🔗
      🔗
    4. The logistic differential equation \(\dfrac{dy}{dt}=ky\left(1-\dfrac{y}{N}\right)\) where (\(k, N > 0 \) are constants) is separable (and autonomous).
      🔗
      🔗
    5. \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\dfrac{\sqrt{y}}{xy+x+y+1}\) is separable.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=y+x\) is not separable. As we will see in Section [cross-reference to target(s) "sec-Linear" missing or not unique], this equation is also linear.
      🔗
      🔗
    7. \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=y^{3}+xy+5\) is not separable.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.2.3 Method of Separation of Variables

    Consider a separable differential equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \dfrac{dy}{dx}=g(x)h(y). \end{equation*}
    Observe that, if there is such a \(c\in \mathbb{R}\) such that \(h(c)=0\text{,}\) then the constant function \(y=c\) is a so-called equilibrium solution of the equation. The method of separation of variables is used for solving these differential equations.
    🔗

    Subsubsection 2.2.3.1 How Separation of Variables Works

    The method of separation of variables works as follows:
    🔗
    1. Find all equilibrium solutions from the equation
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} h(y)=0. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. Separate the variables:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{h(y)}\, dy=g(x)\,dx. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    3. Integrate both sides:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \int\frac{1}{h(y)}\,dy=\int g(x)\,dx. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    4. Solve the resulting equation for \(y\text{,}\) whenever possible, obtaining a general solution. The equilibrium solutions that are not included in this general solution give the singular solutions.
      🔗
      🔗
    Example 2.2.3.
    The separable differential equation
    \begin{equation*} y'=y^{2}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    has a general solution of \(y=\dfrac{1}{c-x}\) ( \(c\in\mathbb{R}\)) and the singular equilibrium solution \(y=0\) not included in the general solution.
    🔗
    🔗
    Remark 2.2.4.
    If it is impossible (or impractical) to solve the resulting equation for \(y\text{,}\) the general solution is said to be an implicit general solution.
    🔗
    🔗
    Example 2.2.5. Solving a Separable Differential Equation.
    (a)
    Solve the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dt}=\frac{2y+1}{t}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsubsection 2.2.3.2 Why Separation of Variables Works

    The method of separation of variables is based on the substitution rule for indefinite integrals [cross-reference to target(s) "Stewart" missing or not unique].
    🔗
    Indeed, let \(y=y(x)\) be a nonequilibrium solution of a separable equation
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \dfrac{dy}{dx}=g(x)h(y)\tag{2.2.3} \end{equation}
    on an interval \(I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Then, for all \(x\in I\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation} y'(x)=g(x)h(y(x)).\tag{2.2.4} \end{equation}
    Dividing both sides of differential equation (2.2.4) by \(h(y(x))\) and integrating, we obtain
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int\frac{1}{h(y(x))}y'(x)\,dx=\int g(x)\,dx \text{ on }I. \end{equation*}
    Using the substitution \(y=y(x)\text{,}\) we obtain
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \int\frac{1}{h(y)}\,dy=\int g(x)\, dx.\tag{2.2.5} \end{equation}
    Thus, any nonequilibrium solution \(y(x)\) of differential equation (2.2.3) satisfies integral equation (2.2.5).
    🔗
    Conversely, a function \(y=y(x)\text{,}\) implicitly defined by integral equation (2.2.5) on the interval \(I\text{,}\) solves (2.2.3) on \(I\text{.}\) Indeed, differentiating (2.2.5) relative to \(x\) by the chain rule (considering \(y=y(x)\) ), we arrive at (2.2.4) for all \(x\in I\text{.}\)
    🔗
