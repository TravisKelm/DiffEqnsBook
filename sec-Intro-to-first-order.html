    Section 2.1 Introduction to First-Order Differential Equations

    Subsection 2.1.1 Particular, General, and Singular Solutions

    Definition 2.1.1. Particular, General, and Singular Solutions.

    A function \(y=y(x)\) is said to be a solution of differential equation (2.0.1) on an interval \(I\) provided it is differentiable on \(I\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'(x)=f\left(x,y(x)\right)\text{ for all }x\in I \end{equation*}
    A one-parameter family of solutions \(y=y(x,c)\) of differential equation (2.0.1) containing an arbitrary constant\(c\) is called a general solution.
    🔗
    Any particular choice of a specific value for \(c\) yields a particular solution.
    🔗
    A first-order differential equation may have both a general solution involving an arbitrary constant\(c\) and particular solutions that cannot be obtained from it by any choice of \(c\text{.}\) Such exceptional solutions are called singular solutions(see Examples [cross-reference to target(s) "example-Singular" missing or not unique] in Section Section 2.2).
    🔗
    To solve (2.0.1) is to find all of its solutions.
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 2.1.2.

    Since a differentiable function is necessarily continuous, every solution \(y=y(x)\) of (2.0.1) on an interval \(I\) must be continuous on \(I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    For a first-order equation of the form
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'=f(x), \end{equation*}
    where \(f(x)\) is a continuous function on an interval \(I\text{,}\) a general solution on \(I\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y=\int f(x)\,dx=F(x)+c\qquad (c\in \mathbb{R}), \end{equation*}
    where \(F(x)\) is an antiderivative of \(f\) on \(I\text{.}\) By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (see, e.g., [cross-reference to target(s) "Stewart" missing or not unique] ), we can set
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F(x):=\int_{x_0}^{x}f(t)\,dt,\quad x\in I, \end{equation*}
    where \(x_{0}\) is a fixed point in \(I\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 2.1.3.

    1. The one-parameter family \(y=c\) ( \(c\in \mathbb{R}\) ) is a general solution of the equation.
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} y'=0 \end{equation*}
      on \((-\infty,\infty)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The one-parameter family \(y=\ln|x|+c\) ( \(c \in \mathbb{R}\) ) is the general solution of the equation.
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} y'=\dfrac{1}{x} \end{equation*}
      on each of the intervals \((-\infty,0)\) and \((0,\infty)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.1.2 Initial Value Problems

    Definition 2.1.4. Initial Value Problem.

    A first-order differential equation (2.0.1) is often accompanied by a condition of the form:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y(x_{0})=y_{0}, \end{equation*}
    where \(x_{0}\) and \(y_{0}\) are specified values. Such a condition is called an initial condition (IC) and such a problem
    🔗
    \begin{equation} y'=f(x,y),\ y(x_{0})=y_{0}\tag{2.1.1} \end{equation}
    is called an initial value problem (IVP).
    🔗
    A solution of the initial value problem (2.1.1) is a function \(y=y(x)\) satisfying both the differential equation on some interval \(I\) containing \(x_{0}\) and the initial condition.
    🔗
    🔗
    One way to solve initial value problem (2.1.1) is to
    🔗
    1. first find the general solution \(y=y(x,c)\) of the equation, which, provided the differential equation is of the form
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} y'=f(x), \end{equation*}
      can be done via indefinite integration
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} y=\int f(x)\,dx, \end{equation*}
      and
      🔗
      🔗
    2. then, plug \(x = x_{0}\) and \(y = y_{0}\) into the general solution, and solve for \(c\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.5.

    Solve the initial value problem
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y'=\dfrac{1}{x},\ y(1)=0. \end{equation*}
    Solution: Considering that the initial \(x\)-value 1 is in the interval \((0,\infty)\text{,}\) where \(|x|=x\text{,}\) we obtain the unique solution of the IVP from the general solution \(y=\ln|x|+c=\ln x+c\) of the equation on \((0,\infty)\) by substituting the values \(x=1\) and \(y=0\) into it:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0=\ln 1+c, \end{equation*}
    which yields \(c = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Thus, the unique solution to the IVP on \((0,\infty)\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \boxed{y=\ln x.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    An alternative definite-integral approach to solving an initial value problem of the form
    🔗
    \begin{equation} y'=f(x),\ y(x_{0})=y_{0},\tag{2.1.2} \end{equation}
    which bypasses finding the general solution of the differential equation, is based on the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus and, provided the function \(f(x)\) is continuous on an interval \(I\) containing \(x_{0}\text{,}\) gives the unique solution of the IVP by the formula
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y=y_{0}+\int_{x_0}^{x}f(t)\,dt. \end{equation*}
    In the following examples, we will compare the two approaches to solving initial value problem (2.1.2).
    🔗
    1. \(y'=x\sqrt{x^{2}+9},\ y(-4)=0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      Solution: (indefinite integral approach)
      🔗
      First, we find the general solution of the equation
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} y= \int x \sqrt{x^{2} + 9}\, dx \end{equation*}
      Using substitution (\(u = x^{2} + 9\text{,}\) so \(du = 2x \, dx\)), we obtain
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} y= \frac{1}{3}(x^{2} + 9)^{\frac{3}{2}}+ C \end{equation*}
      Next, we need to determine the value of \(c\) for which the solution we have obtained will satisfy the IVP.
      🔗
      Substituting in the initial condition gives
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \dfrac{1}{3}\left((-4)^{2}+9\right)^{3/2}+c=0, \end{equation*}
      🔗
      which we solve to find \(c=-\dfrac{125}{3}\text{.}\) The unique solution is, therefore,
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \boxed{y = \dfrac{1}{3}(x^2+9)^{3/2}-\dfrac{125}{3},} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      or
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \boxed{y = \dfrac{1}{3}\left((x^2+9)^{3/2}-125\right).} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(y'=xe^{-x},\ y(0)=1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      Solution: (definite-integral approach)
      🔗
      The desired solution is given by the formula
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} y=1+\int_{0}^{x}te^{-t}\,dt. \end{equation*}
      Integrating by parts, with
      🔗
      \begin{align*} u\amp =t, du\amp =dt\\ dv\amp= e^{-t}\,dt, v\amp =-e^{-t}, \end{align*}
      we have:
      🔗
      \begin{align*} \int_{0}^{x}te^{-t}\,dt\amp = \left[\left.-te^{-t}\right|_{0}^{x}+\int_{0}^{x} e^{-t}\,dt\right]\\ \amp =\left[\left.-xe^{-x}-e^{-t}\right|_{0}^{x}\right] \\ \amp =\left[-xe^{-x}-(e^{-x}-1)\right]\\ \amp =-xe^{-x}-e^{-x}+1=-(x+1)e^{-x}+1. \end{align*}
      Therefore, the solution is
      \begin{equation*} \boxed{y(x)=-(x+1)e^{-x}+2.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
